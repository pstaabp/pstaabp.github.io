Chapter 13: Curvature of Plane curves

An interesting topic related to derivatives is that of the curvature of a curve at a point, called \(\kappa\), the greek letter kappa, which is \[ \kappa = \frac{|f^{\prime\prime}(a)|}{(1+f'(a)^{2})^{3/2}} \]

Geometrically, the curvature is \[ \kappa = \frac{1}{R}\] where \(R\) is the radius of the circle that is tangent to the curve at the point \((a,f(a))\)

As an example, let’s take \(f(x)=x^{2}\) and find the curvature at \(x=0\). Plugging into Maple, we get: \(\kappa = 2\). Thus the radius of the circle that is tangent to this is \(R=1/2\) and since \(x=0\), then the circle is \(x^{2}+(y-1/2)^{2} =(1/2)^{2}\). A plot of this is

Example

Find the tangent circle to the curve \(y=x^{2}\) at \(x=1\). Plot the curve and the circle.

The curvature in this case is \(\kappa = 2\sqrt{5}/25\). To find the circle, it is a bit more challenging since it is not clear where the center of the circle is. The center of the circle will be on the line that is perpendicular to the curve at the point. Since \(f'(a)=2a\), the perpendicular line is \[ y=-\frac{1}{2}(x-1)+1 \]

To find the center of the circle, we seek the intersection of the this line and the circle \((x-1)^{2}+(y-1)^{2} = (25/(2\sqrt{5})^{2}\), since the distance along the line is \(1/\kappa\). If we let \(a=1\), then

solve([y = -1/(f'(a))*(x-a)+f(a), (x-a)^2+(y-f(a))^2 = 1/kappa(a)^2])

returns

{x = 6, y = -3/2}, {x = -4, y = 7/2}
